There are two primary experiences that have shaped why and how I make friends.

The first is simply a fact: I am an only child, and I never went to kindergarten. Since the very beginning, I learned to entertain myself. Ever since I learned how to read, the easiest way to find me was to find the nearest corner; most likely, I would be there with yet another book. I liked to read anything adventurous, be it *Tom Sawyer* or *Oliver Twist*. I still love the fascinating imaginary worlds.

In principle, I do not feel as strong a need for companionship. Do not get me wrong: I love shared experiences. But I am also entirely comfortable doing just about anything by myself; silence helps the everyday tension and the burden of an overstimulated nervous system leave my body.

The second is circumstance: I have lived away from home since I was fifteen (I am twenty-five at the moment of writing this). Very often, with my family far away, I could only rely on myself. Speaking from a highly privileged position, I still had to manage the endless documents, permits, side jobs, moves, and bureaucratic complications.

This, and the fact above, created a strange state in which I became abominably independent, yet often felt lonely. Sometimes I still do, although much less than before.

Some years ago, somewhere in between yet another move, I figured out that I need friends. Not for the companionship of it. For the *truth* of it.

I enjoy friendly banter: chatting about films, sports, books — whatever comes to mind. Yet the thing I value most in friendship is learning what people truly care about in life; what their aspirations are; who they are.

It matters to me to know, with material concerns stripped aside and ordinary needs satisfied, *who* we would choose to be. What kind of people we are outside of daily work and social roleplaying. What values, if nobody watched, we would stand for. More than anything else, this gives me a sense of true connection — a feeling that life is not lived foolishly.

Quite bluntly, I do not think I care how many times we partied together. If there was even one conversation in which I was trusted with the vulnerable fact of what truly matters in life to you, I will appreciate it. I promise that I will treat this knowledge with care and respect. That I will be gentle with it, and with *us* — the *us* that emerges in that exact moment. It is rare; it deserves protection.

And if you want, I will share what I truly care about too.
